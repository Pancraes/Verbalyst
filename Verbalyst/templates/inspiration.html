The Story Behind Verbalyst

When the quarantine was introduced, many individuals, including us, were starved of social interactions. It was difficult transitioning from text-only interactions to fully-fledged conversations after the pandemic, let alone public speeches and presentations. Although enough time has passed to mend our abilities to speak to others and have fruitful conversations, speeches in front of large groups still stand as challenges that we'd rather not face. Knowing that the prevalence of social anxiety is increasing drastically in our society, we decided to tackle the issue that we and many others face with our app, Verbalyst.


But what even are speech impediments?

There are several, but the main ones that Verbalyst looks for are stuttering and lisps.

Stuttering:

Stuttering, also known as a stutter, is a relatively common speech disorder characterized by disruptions in the flow of speech. It often involves repetitions, prolongations, or blocks on sounds or words. Here are some statistics:

  • Prevalence: Stuttering affects about 1% of the global population.
  • Age of Onset: Stuttering typically starts between the ages of 2 and 7, often during a child's early language development.
  • Gender: Stuttering is more common in males than females, with a ratio of approximately 3:1.
  • Natural Recovery: Many children who stutter during their early years naturally recover without any intervention. For others, it may persist into adulthood.

Lisps:

Lisps are speech disorders characterized by difficulties in producing specific speech sounds, particularly "s" and "z" sounds. Here are some general statistics:

  • Prevalence: The prevalence of lisps can vary depending on the type and severity of the lisp. It's important to note that not all speech sound errors involving "s" and "z" are classified as lisps; some may be typical developmental errors.
  • Developmental vs. Articulatory Lisps: Developmental lisps are common in young children and usually resolve with age and speech development. Articulatory lisps, caused by physical differences in the oral cavity, are less common and may require intervention.

General tips:

Improving speech and communication skills is something that anyone can work on to enhance their overall communication effectiveness. Here are some tips and steps that an average person can follow to work on improving their speech:

Practice Clear Pronunciation:

Focus on pronouncing words clearly and correctly. Pay attention to sounds you find challenging and practice saying them correctly.

Slow Down Your Speech:

Speaking too quickly can lead to unclear speech. Practice speaking at a moderate pace to ensure that your words are enunciated properly.

Record Yourself:

Use a voice recorder or smartphone to record yourself speaking. Listen to the recordings and identify areas where you can improve clarity or pronunciation.

Read Aloud:

Regularly read books, articles, or passages aloud. This practice can help improve your articulation, rhythm, and overall speaking fluency.

Expand Vocabulary:

A wider vocabulary allows you to choose the most appropriate words for different contexts, making your speech more engaging and effective.

Listen Actively:

Pay attention to how others speak. Listen to people with clear and expressive speech, and try to emulate their patterns.

Engage in Conversations:

Engage in conversations with diverse groups of people. This can help you adapt your speech to different styles and improve your ability to communicate effectively with various individuals.

Seek Feedback:

Ask friends, family members, or colleagues for constructive feedback on your speech. They can help identify areas for improvement that you might not notice yourself.

Use Online Resources:

There are numerous online resources, including videos, articles, and exercises, that can provide guidance on improving speech clarity, pronunciation, and communication skills.

Consider Professional Help:

If you have persistent concerns about your speech, consider consulting a speech-language pathologist (speech therapist). They can provide personalized assessment, guidance, and exercises to address specific areas of improvement.

Practice Breathing and Relaxation Techniques:

Good breath control and relaxation can positively impact your speech. Practice deep breathing and relaxation exercises to enhance your overall vocal control.

Stay Hydrated:

Adequate hydration is important for maintaining healthy vocal cords. Drink enough water throughout the day to support clear and comfortable speech.

Remember, improving speech is a gradual process that takes time and consistent effort. The goal is not to eliminate uniqueness from your speech but to enhance clarity and effectiveness. Celebrate your progress and embrace your individuality as you work on becoming a more confident and effective communicator.
